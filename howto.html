Real-world tips to stay ahead of attackers

How to Avoid Hackers, Hacks & Phishing Attacks

Most attacks don’t start with “movie-style” hacking. They start with a tricked click, a weak password, or a rushed decision. Use this page as your quick, practical checklist for staying safe online at home and at work.

Rule of thumb: if something feels urgent, emotional, or “too good to be true,” pause and verify using another trusted method.

Core Steps to Avoid Hackers & Hacks

Bulleted checklist
  • 1
    Be ruthless with suspicious emails & texts

    Never click links or open attachments from messages you weren’t expecting. Hover over links to check the real URL, and watch for misspellings, odd senders, or urgent language (“act now or your account will be closed!”).

  • 2
    Use strong, unique passwords everywhere

    Create long passphrases (e.g., four+ random words) and do not reuse the same password across sites. A password manager can generate and store unique passwords so you only remember one master password.

  • 3
    Turn on multi-factor authentication (MFA)

    Wherever possible, enable MFA (codes via app, text, or hardware key). Even if hackers steal your password, MFA makes it much harder for them to log in.

  • 4
    Keep software & devices updated

    Turn on automatic updates for your operating system, browser, and apps. Many attacks exploit known holes that updates have already fixed.

  • 5
    Lock down your accounts’ recovery options

    Check that your backup email, phone numbers, and security questions are current and secure. Avoid obvious answers (like your mother’s real maiden name) that can be found on social media.

  • 6
    Be careful on public Wi-Fi

    On public networks (cafés, airports, hotels), assume others could see your traffic. Avoid logging into sensitive accounts unless you’re using a VPN or your own mobile hotspot.

  • 7
    Review your accounts & devices regularly

    Check bank, email, and social media activity often. Look for logins from unknown locations/devices and sign out of sessions you don’t recognize; then change your password.

  • 8
    Back up important data securely

    Keep copies of key files in at least two places (for example, encrypted cloud storage and an external drive you disconnect when not in use). Backups help you recover from ransomware or device loss.

  • 9
    Slow down before you click or pay

    Hackers rely on urgency. If a message demands money, gift cards, or crypto right now, stop. Verify using a phone number or website you find yourself, not the one in the message.

  • 10
    Ask for help when something feels off

    If you’re unsure whether something is legit, ask a trusted person or your IT/security team. It’s always better to double-check than to clean up a breach later.
