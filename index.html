Hackers typically target individuals not because of who they are, but because of what they represent: an opportunity. From the hacker’s perspective, a person with weak security—such as reused passwords, outdated software, or unprotected public Wi-Fi use—is simply an easy entry point. Their goal may be financial gain, such as stealing payment information or selling personal data, or they may want to quietly add the victim’s device to a botnet they control. In many cases, hacking is completely impersonal and driven by automation, where software scans thousands of accounts to find vulnerabilities, meaning the victim wasn’t singled out but rather “caught” by weak defenses. At times, curiosity, challenge, or personal motives—such as an ex-partner or disgruntled acquaintance—can also drive a breach. Ultimately, from a hacker’s viewpoint, someone is hacked because their digital guard was down, their information had value, or their device could serve a larger objective.
